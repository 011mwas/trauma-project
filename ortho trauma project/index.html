    Frequently Asked Questions

    Find answers to common questions about our orthopaedic services, injury treatment, and well-being.

    Common signs of a fracture include sudden, severe pain, swelling, bruising, deformity or an unnatural shape of the limb, difficulty using or moving the injured area, or even bone protruding through the skin in severe cases (open fracture). If you suspect a fracture, seek medical attention immediately.

    You should consider seeing an orthopaedic specialist if you experience persistent joint pain lasting more than a few days, pain that worsens with activity or wakes you at night, noticeable swelling or stiffness in a joint, a decrease in your range of motion, or instability in a joint (feeling like it might "give way").

    A sprain is an injury to a ligament (the tough bands of tissue that connect bones to each other at a joint). It often occurs from a sudden twist or wrench. A strain is an injury to a muscle or a tendon (the cords that attach muscles to bones). Strains are often caused by overstretching or overusing a muscle.

    R.I.C.E. is an acronym for a common first-aid treatment for many soft tissue injuries like sprains and strains:

    • Rest: Avoid using the injured area.
    • Ice: Apply ice packs for 15-20 minutes at a time, several times a day, to reduce swelling and pain.
    • Compression: Use an elastic bandage to wrap the injured area to help reduce swelling (be careful not to wrap too tightly).
    • Elevation: Keep the injured part elevated above the level of your heart to help reduce swelling.

The need for surgery depends on several factors, including the specific ligament torn, the severity of the tear (partial or complete), your activity level, your age, and whether other structures in the joint are also injured. Many ligament tears can be managed non-surgically with rest, physical therapy, and bracing. Your orthopaedic specialist will recommend the best course of treatment for your specific situation.

    You can reduce your risk of sports injuries by:

    • Warming up properly before activity and cooling down afterward.
    • Using proper techniques and form for your sport.
    • Gradually increasing the intensity and duration of your training.
    • Wearing appropriate protective gear (e.g., helmets, pads).
    • Using the correct footwear for your activity.
    • Listening to your body and resting when you feel pain or fatigue.
    • Cross-training to avoid overusing specific muscle groups.
    • Staying hydrated and maintaining a balanced diet.
